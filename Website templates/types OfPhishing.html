1. Spear phishing

Spear phishing involves targeting a specific individual in an organization to try to steal their credentials. The attacker often first gathers information about the person before starting the attack, such as their name, position, and contact details.

2. Watering hole phishing

In a watering hole phishing attack, a hacker figures out a site a group of users tends to visit. They then use it to infect the computers of the users in an attempt to penetrate the network and spread to other computers.

3. Evil twin phishing

In an evil twin attack, the hacker sets up a false Wi-Fi network that looks real. If someone logs in to it and enters sensitive details, the hacker captures their info.

4. Pop-up phishing

Pop-up phishing often uses a pop-up about a problem with the security of your computer or some other issue to trick you into clicking. You are then directed to download a file, which ends up being malware, or to call what is supposed to be a support center.

5. HTTPS phishing

An HTTPS phishing attack is carried out by sending the victim an email with a link to a fake website. The site may then be used to dupe the victim into entering their private information.

6. Email phishing

In an email phishing scam, the attacker sends an email that looks legitimate, designed to trick the recipient into entering information in reply or on a site that the hacker can use to steal or sell their data.

Vishing

This is a type of phishing which is done via phone, calling the user with a different voice to trick them into giving money or sensitive data.

Whaling

This is another type of spear fishing in which high-end executives such as CEO 's and CFO's are targeted, as they have large amounts of money and sensitive data. Usually, hackers research a lot about the person beforehand and create highly personalized emails to convince the recipient thoroughly.

Vishing

Vishing, or voice phishing, is a form of cyber attack that involves the unauthorized use of a fraudulent call or voice message to entice the victim to reveal confidential information. They may call in as a bank, someone from technical support, or even portray an agency of the government just for the purpose of making him/her feel urgently needed, thereby making the victim spill private details.
